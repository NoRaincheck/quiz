# Options Trading for Beginners: Call Options Study Guide

## Introduction

Options trading can seem complex, but understanding the basics of call options is a great starting
point. This guide covers the fundamental concepts you need to know.

---

## The Three Key Option Characteristics

### 1. Expiration Date

All options have an expiration date. Unlike shares of stock (which you can hold forever), options
expire at a specific time. This could be days, weeks, months, or up to 2.5 years from purchase.
After expiration, the option ceases to exist.

### 2. Strike Price

The strike price is the price at which the option can be converted into shares of stock. For
example, if you have a call option with a strike price of $105, you can buy shares at $105
regardless of the current market price.

### 3. Contract Multiplier

Each option contract typically represents 100 shares of stock. This means:

- If an option price is shown as $5, the actual cost is $5 × 100 = $500
- If an option price is $81.70, the actual cost is $81.70 × 100 = $8,170

## What is a Call Option?

A call option gives the buyer the **right to buy 100 shares** of stock at the strike price between
now and the expiration date.

**Key characteristics of call options:**

- Call option prices increase as the stock price increases
- Call option prices decrease as the stock price decreases
- The price of call options moves with the stock price

```quiz
What does a call option give the buyer the right to do?

  - [ ] Sell 100 shares at the strike price
  - [x] Buy 100 shares at the strike price
  - [ ] Buy 100 shares at the market price

  A call option gives the buyer the right to BUY shares at the strike price. A put option would give the right to sell.

---

If you buy a call option and the stock price decreases, what happens to the call option's price?

  - [ ] It increases
  - [ ] It stays the same
  - [x] It decreases

  Call option prices move in the same direction as the stock price. When the stock goes down, call options become less valuable.
```

## Real-World Example: Tesla Call Option

**Setup:**

- Tesla stock price: $811
- Call option strike price: $800
- Expiration: 39 days away
- Option price: $81.70
- **Actual premium cost: $8,170** (81.70 × 100)

**Scenario 1: Stock rises to $1,000**

- You can buy 100 shares at $800 (strike price)
- Market value of those shares: $100,000
- Cost to buy: $80,000
- **Gross profit: $20,000**
- **Net profit: $11,830** (after subtracting $8,170 premium)

**Scenario 2: Stock falls to $150**

- You would NOT exercise the option
- **Maximum loss: $8,170** (the premium paid)

```quiz
If an option is priced at $5.00 and the contract multiplier is 100, what is the actual cost to buy one contract?

  - [ ] $5.00
  - [ ] $50.00
  - [x] $500.00

  Multiply the option price by 100 (the contract multiplier) to get the actual premium cost: $5.00 × 100 = $500.

---

In the Tesla example, what was the maximum loss if the stock price fell?

  - [ ] $80,000
  - [ ] $100,000
  - [x] $8,170

  The maximum loss on a long option position is the premium paid. You simply don't exercise the option if it's not profitable.
```

## Important Concepts

### You Don't Have to Exercise the Option

99% of the time, traders profit by **selling the option itself** at a higher price, not by
exercising it. The option's price already reflects any potential profit from exercising.

### Leverage

Options provide leveraged returns. In the Tesla example:

- Stock price increased: **3%**
- Call option value increased: **15%**
- The option return was **5x higher** than the stock return

### Limited Risk, Unlimited Upside

- **Maximum loss**: The premium paid for the option
- **Potential gain**: Can be substantial if the stock moves favorably

```quiz

  - [x] They profit by selling the option at a higher price instead

  The option's price already includes the value of any potential exercise profit. Traders can sell the option to realize gains without the complexity of exercising.
```
